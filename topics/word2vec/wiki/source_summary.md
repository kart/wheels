## High-Level Paper Claim Map

The paper proposes two model architectures for learning continuous word vector representations from very large text corpora:

- Continuous Bag-of-Words, abbreviated CBOW
- Continuous Skip-gram

The paper's central motivation is that many NLP systems treated words as atomic vocabulary indices, which is simple and robust but does not encode word similarity. The authors argue that simple scaling of traditional techniques is insufficient in some settings, and that distributed word representations can help.

The paper's central engineering claim is that simpler log-linear architectures can learn useful high-dimensional word vectors at much lower computational cost than heavier neural language models. This lower cost makes it feasible to train on much larger corpora and vocabularies.

The paper evaluates learned vectors using syntactic and semantic regularity questions, including analogy-style vector operations. The authors report that CBOW and Skip-gram achieve strong accuracy compared with prior neural models, with Skip-gram especially strong on semantic questions in several reported comparisons.

Important caution for later lesson writing:

- The paper does not claim to invent continuous or distributed word representations. It explicitly says these have a long history and cites prior work.
- Vector arithmetic examples should be presented as empirical regularities of trained vectors, not as guaranteed symbolic reasoning.
- The paper's strongest claims are bounded by its tasks, datasets, and exact-match evaluation metric.

## Section-by-Section Source Map

### Abstract

Source claims:

- The authors propose two new model architectures for continuous word representations from very large datasets.
- They measure representation quality using word similarity tasks and compare against earlier neural-network-based techniques.
- They report large accuracy improvements at lower computational cost.
- They state that high-quality vectors can be learned from a 1.6 billion word dataset in less than a day.
- They report state-of-the-art performance on their syntactic and semantic word similarity test set.

Teaching use:

- Good source for the overall promise: better vectors, cheaper training, large data.
- Avoid opening the beginner lesson with performance claims alone; first explain why word vectors are needed.

### 1. Introduction

Source claims:

- Many NLP systems represented words as atomic units, with no notion of similarity between words.
- Atomic word representations have practical advantages: simplicity, robustness, and compatibility with large-scale simple models such as n-grams.
- The authors argue that simple scaling has limits in tasks where relevant high-quality data is limited, such as speech recognition and machine translation.
- They identify distributed word representations as a successful concept and cite neural-network-based language models as outperforming n-gram models.

Teaching use:

- Use this section to motivate one-hot or index-style word representations before introducing dense vectors.
- Explain "atomic" as "word identity only": the model knows `cat` and `dog` are different IDs, but not that they are semantically related.

### 1.1 Goals of the Paper

Source claims:

- The paper aims to learn high-quality word vectors from billions of words and vocabularies with millions of words.
- The authors say previous architectures had not, to their knowledge, been successfully trained beyond a few hundred million words with only modest dimensionality.
- The desired vectors should support multiple degrees of similarity, not just nearest-neighbor similarity.
- The paper uses word offset operations, including the famous `King - Man + Woman` example from prior work, as motivation for preserving linear regularities.
- The authors design a comprehensive test set for syntactic and semantic regularities.
- The paper studies how training time and accuracy depend on vector dimensionality and amount of training data.

Teaching use:

- Introduce vector offsets only after the reader understands vectors as learned points.
- Make clear that the paper is trying to improve accuracy of these operations, not merely show amusing examples.

### 1.2 Previous Work

Source claims:

- Continuous vector representations of words have a long history.
- The paper discusses feedforward neural network language models with projection and hidden layers.
- It cites earlier work where word vectors were learned first and then used in an NNLM.
- The authors directly extend that simpler two-step direction and focus on learning word vectors.
- Existing word vectors had been used to improve or simplify NLP applications.
- The authors characterize most prior architectures as more computationally expensive than the architecture they extend, except for some log-bilinear variants.

Teaching use:

- Use this section to prevent overclaiming historical novelty.
- Frame Word2Vec as a practical architecture/scaling contribution in an ongoing line of representation-learning work.

### 2. Model Architectures

Source claims:

- The paper focuses on distributed word representations learned by neural networks.
- It compares architectures using computational complexity: the number of parameters that must be accessed to fully train the model.
- The authors want to maximize accuracy while minimizing computational complexity.

Equation:

- Equation (1): `O = E x T x Q`
- Meaning from the paper:
  - `E`: number of training epochs
  - `T`: number of words in the training set
  - `Q`: architecture-specific cost per training example

Teaching use:

- Explain Equation (1) as bookkeeping: total work equals passes through data times number of tokens times work per token.
- Do not start with this equation; use it after a concrete training example.

### 2.1 Feedforward Neural Net Language Model

Source claims:

- The feedforward NNLM has input, projection, hidden, and output layers.
- It encodes `N` previous words with `1-of-V` coding, where `V` is vocabulary size.
- The projection layer has dimensionality `N x D`, using a shared projection matrix.
- The expensive part is computation between dense projection and hidden layers, plus output over the vocabulary.
- Hierarchical softmax or other strategies can reduce the full-vocabulary output cost.
- The paper uses hierarchical softmax with a Huffman binary tree, where frequent words get shorter codes.

Equation:

- Equation (2): `Q = N x D + N x D x H + H x V`
- The paper identifies `H x V` as a dominating term before output optimizations, and says much remaining complexity comes from `N x D x H`.

Teaching use:

- Use NNLM only as contrast. For beginners, avoid going deeply into all NNLM mechanics unless needed to explain why CBOW/Skip-gram are cheaper.
- Explain hierarchical softmax as reducing "score every word" into "walk a tree path."

### 2.2 Recurrent Neural Net Language Model

Source claims:

- RNNLMs address feedforward NNLM limitations such as fixed context length.
- RNNs use time-delayed recurrent connections in the hidden layer to represent information from the past.
- The model has input, hidden, and output layers, but no projection layer in the description here.

Equation:

- Equation (3): `Q = H x H + H x V`
- With hierarchical softmax, `H x V` can be reduced to `H x log2(V)`, leaving `H x H` as much of the complexity.

Teaching use:

- Use RNNLM as a second contrast point: more expressive sequence modeling but still computationally heavy.
- Avoid making the lesson about RNNs; only include enough to support the paper's comparison.

### 2.3 Parallel Training of Neural Networks

Source claims:

- The authors implemented several models on Google's DistBelief distributed framework.
- The system runs multiple replicas in parallel and synchronizes gradient updates through centralized servers.
- It uses mini-batch asynchronous gradient descent with Adagrad.
- The paper says using 100 or more replicas, each with many CPU cores, was common in this framework.

Teaching use:

- Use this later, in the results section, to explain why the paper cares about engineering scalability.
- For beginners, define distributed training only at a high level.

### 3. New Log-linear Models

Source claims:

- The authors propose two new architectures intended to minimize computational complexity.
- The main observation is that much complexity in earlier neural models comes from the non-linear hidden layer.
- The authors explore simpler models that may represent data less precisely but can train on much more data efficiently.
- The architectures follow earlier work where word vectors are learned first and an NNLM is trained on top later.

Teaching use:

- This is the conceptual pivot of the lesson: remove expensive hidden-layer machinery and train word vectors through simple prediction tasks.

### 3.1 Continuous Bag-of-Words Model

Source claims:

- CBOW is similar to a feedforward NNLM with the non-linear hidden layer removed.
- The projection layer is shared for all words; context word vectors are averaged because all words are projected into the same position.
- Word order in the history does not influence the projection.
- The model also uses future words, not just previous words.
- The paper reports best performance for a log-linear classifier with four future and four history words as input, predicting the current middle word.
- The architecture is shown in Figure 1.

Equation:

- Equation (4): `Q = N x D + D x log2(V)`

Teaching use:

- Teach CBOW as "use surrounding words to guess the missing middle word."
- Emphasize that CBOW ignores order in this architecture, which is why it is a bag-of-words-style model.

### 3.2 Continuous Skip-gram Model

Source claims:

- Skip-gram is similar to CBOW but reverses the prediction direction.
- It uses the current word as input and predicts words within a range before and after it.
- Increasing the range can improve vector quality but increases computational complexity.
- More distant words are sampled less often because they are usually less related to the current word.
- In the experiments, the paper uses `C = 10`.

Equation:

- Equation (5): `Q = C x (D + D x log2(V))`
- `C` is the maximum distance of words.

Teaching use:

- Teach Skip-gram as "use one center word to guess its neighbors."
- Carefully avoid reversing CBOW and Skip-gram in later artifacts.

### 4. Results

Source claims:

- The paper argues that looking only at nearest-neighbor examples is insufficient for evaluating vector quality.
- It frames analogy-style questions as a harder similarity task.
- Example task: find the word similar to `small` in the same sense that `biggest` is similar to `big`.
- The paper computes `vector("biggest") - vector("big") + vector("small")`, then searches by cosine distance and discards input question words.
- The correct answer in the example is `smallest`.
- The paper says high-dimensional vectors trained on large data can answer subtle semantic relationships, such as country-capital relationships.

Teaching use:

- Use this section to explain evaluation after the reader understands vectors.
- Explain exact-match scoring and why synonyms can be counted as wrong.

### 4.1 Task Description

Source claims:

- The Semantic-Syntactic Word Relationship test set contains five semantic question types and nine syntactic question types.
- The paper reports 8,869 semantic and 10,675 syntactic questions.
- Questions were created by manually making lists of similar word pairs and connecting pairs to form questions.
- The test set includes only single-token words, excluding multi-word entities such as "New York."
- A question is correct only if the closest computed vector is exactly the same as the expected answer.
- Synonyms are counted as mistakes.
- The paper says 100% accuracy is likely impossible because the models lack input information about word morphology.

### 4.2 Maximization of Accuracy

Source claims:

- The authors used Google News data with about 6B tokens and restricted vocabulary to the 1 million most frequent words.
- They first evaluated models on training-data subsets with vocabulary restricted to the 30k most frequent words.
- They report that after some point, increasing only dimensions or only training data gives diminishing improvements.
- The paper says data amount and vector dimensionality should be increased together.
- The authors note that, from Equation (4), doubling training data is roughly like doubling vector size in computational cost.
- For Tables 2 and 4, the authors used three epochs of stochastic gradient descent and backpropagation, starting learning rate 0.025, linearly decreased toward zero.

Table:

- Table 2: CBOW accuracy on a subset of the Semantic-Syntactic test set with limited vocabulary.
- Dimensions shown: 50, 100, 300, 600.
- Training-word subsets shown: 24M, 49M, 98M, 196M, 391M, 783M.
- Accuracy trend: larger vector dimensionality and more training data generally improve accuracy, with diminishing returns.
- Notable endpoint from extracted table: 600-dimensional vectors trained on 783M words reach 50.4 accuracy on this subset.

Visual/table inspection required:

- Yes before final charting. The table is readable, but a chart should be created from checked numeric values.

### 4.3 Comparison of Model Architectures

Source claims:

- The paper compares RNNLM, NNLM, CBOW, and Skip-gram using the same data and 640-dimensional vectors.
- It also evaluates on a syntactic relationship test set from prior work.
- The data for this comparison consists of several LDC corpora: 320M words and 82K vocabulary.
- A previously trained RNNLM took about 8 weeks on a single CPU.
- The NNLM used DistBelief parallel training with a history of 8 previous words.
- The paper states that CBOW works better than NNLM on syntactic tasks and about the same on semantic tasks.
- Skip-gram is slightly worse than CBOW on syntactic tasks but much better on semantic tasks in Table 3.

Tables:

- Table 3: architecture comparison with 640-dimensional vectors.
  - RNNLM: semantic 9, syntactic 36, MSR syntactic test 35.
  - NNLM: semantic 23, syntactic 53, MSR syntactic test 47.
  - CBOW: semantic 24, syntactic 64, MSR syntactic test 61.
  - Skip-gram: semantic 55, syntactic 59, MSR syntactic test 56.
- Table 4: comparison against publicly available vectors and the authors' models.
  - Best total shown in Table 4: Skip-gram, 300 dimensions, 783M words, semantic 50.0, syntactic 55.9, total 53.3.

  - One-epoch Skip-gram, 600 dimensions, 783M words: total 55.5, training time 2.5 days.
  - One-epoch Skip-gram, 300 dimensions, 1.6B words: total 53.8, training time 2 days.

### 4.4 Large Scale Parallel Training of Models

Source claims:

- The authors used DistBelief with 50 to 100 replicas and Adagrad on Google News 6B data.
- CPU-core counts are estimates because datacenter machines were shared with production tasks.
- Distributed framework overhead makes CBOW and Skip-gram CPU usage closer than in single-machine implementations.

Table:

- Table 6: DistBelief comparison.
  - NNLM, 100 dimensions, 6B words: semantic 34.2, syntactic 64.5, total 50.8, training time 14 days x 180 CPU cores.
  - CBOW, 1000 dimensions, 6B words: semantic 57.3, syntactic 68.9, total 63.7, training time 2 days x 140 CPU cores.
  - Skip-gram, 1000 dimensions, 6B words: semantic 66.1, syntactic 65.1, total 65.6, training time 2.5 days x 125 CPU cores.

Teaching use:

- Strong evidence for the paper's "scalable training" story.
- Explain that CPU-core/day comparisons are approximate in this table.

### 4.5 Microsoft Research Sentence Completion Challenge

Source claims:

- The challenge has 1040 sentences, each with one missing word and five candidate choices.
- Skip-gram was trained with 640-dimensional vectors on 50M words provided in the challenge.
- The sentence score is computed by using the unknown word as input and predicting all surrounding words in the sentence.
- Skip-gram alone scores 48.0 accuracy, below some previous approaches.
- Combining Skip-gram scores with RNNLMs gives 58.9 accuracy, which the authors report as a new state-of-the-art result at the time.

Table:

- Table 7: sentence completion comparison.
  - 4-gram: 39
  - Average LSA similarity: 49
  - Log-bilinear model: 54.8
  - RNNLMs: 55.4
  - Skip-gram: 48.0
  - Skip-gram + RNNLMs: 58.9

Teaching use:

- This is a secondary result. Use it to show vectors can complement other language models, not as the main lesson thread.

### 6. Conclusion

Source claims:

- The paper concludes that high-quality word vectors can be trained using simple architectures compared with feedforward and recurrent neural network models.
- The lower computational complexity allows accurate high-dimensional vectors to be computed from much larger datasets.
- The authors claim DistBelief should make it possible to train CBOW and Skip-gram on corpora with one trillion words and essentially unlimited vocabulary size.
- The paper expects high-quality word vectors to become an important building block for future NLP applications.
- The authors mention applications and related areas: SemEval-2012 Task 2, sentiment analysis, paraphrase detection, knowledge base extension and verification, and machine translation.

Teaching use:

- Use conclusion claims carefully; some are forward-looking expectations, not completed experiments in the paper.

Source claims:

- They report training speed in the order of billions of words per hour for typical hyperparameters.

## Core Claims to Preserve

- Words represented as atomic indices have no built-in similarity.
- Distributed continuous word vectors can encode useful similarity patterns.
- The paper proposes CBOW and Skip-gram as simpler architectures for learning vectors efficiently.
- CBOW predicts a current/middle word from surrounding context.
- Skip-gram predicts surrounding words from the current word.
- Much of the efficiency story comes from avoiding expensive non-linear hidden layers and using hierarchical softmax.
- Vector quality is evaluated with semantic and syntactic analogy-style questions using cosine nearest-neighbor search.
- Larger vectors and more training data generally help, but they must be balanced against compute.
- Skip-gram is stronger on semantic analogies in the paper's key architecture comparisons; CBOW is fast and performs strongly on syntactic questions.
- Exact-match analogy accuracy is strict and can count synonyms or plausible alternatives as mistakes.

## Claims Requiring Careful Wording

- "State of the art": The paper uses this phrase for its test set and the Microsoft Sentence Completion combination result. In the lesson, tie the phrase to the paper's time and task.
- "Semantic meaning": The paper demonstrates regularities in vector space. Do not imply the vectors understand meaning like a human.
- "King - Man + Woman = Queen": The paper cites this as prior observation and motivation, not as the paper's only or main result.
- "One trillion words" and "unlimited vocabulary": In the conclusion this is a forward-looking scalability claim about what should be possible with DistBelief, not a completed reported experiment.
- "Word2Vec invented embeddings": Do not say this. The paper explicitly cites a long history of continuous vector representations.

## Beginner Teaching Anchors from the Source

Use these as source-grounded bridges into later wiki and lesson work:

- Atomic word IDs are easy but cannot express similarity.
- A context window creates training examples from ordinary text.
- CBOW is a "fill in the middle word" game.
- Skip-gram is a "guess nearby words" game.
- Hierarchical softmax turns a huge word-choice problem into a tree-walking problem.
- Analogy questions test whether similar relationships become similar vector offsets.
- Exact-match scores are useful but imperfect.
